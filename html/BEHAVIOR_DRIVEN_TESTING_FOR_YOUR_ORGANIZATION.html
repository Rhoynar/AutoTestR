Behavior Driven Testing for Your Organization

Software developers are leaning towards using BDD or Behavior Driven Development for testing purposes more readily than the more traditionally accepted methodology, TDD or Test-driven development. Behavior Driven Testing is not only the latest methodology in testing and continuous delivery; but also because it provides end-to-end control and tracking, early fault detection and tighter integration cycle. BDD comes with many tools specifically designed for acceptance testing, here we will go over few tools and evaluate how BDD can help your organization.

BDD comes with its very own set of tools designed specifically for acceptance testing, which makes it popular when it comes to both big as well as small business organizations. Moreover, BDD solves the problem of authenticated automated testing requirements that many developers were looking for. It is also cost-effective due the open source and free frameworks like Cucumber, Specflow and RSpec that it relies on. However, the thing that makes it a favorite amongst novice coders and program managers is the fact that the programming language that BDD is written in, Gherkin, reads very much like the common English language that makes understanding its programs a cakewalk.

A collaborative effort – Program Management works with the Dev/QA Organization

One of the long-standing problems in the organization has been that of requirements misunderstanding. One of my favorite jokes on Requirements Misunderstanding comes from here: where Santa Claus adopts agile practices for toy production. Its a fun 10 minute read.. take your time, we’ll wait here.

Dilbert on Agile

Many organizations claim to follow agile practices, but what they are indeed doing is removing documentation and requirements management from their process-flow and putting more focus on implementation. This may be good in the short run, but in the long run, it can quickly lead to a huge technical-debt buildup causing delays and frustrations for minor work items.

I have worked with many scrum-masters and program-managers and one of the reasons they give me for lack of program management is the fact that requirements are misunderstood or misinterpreted by the developer and there is no accountability at the end of the day.

This is where a programming based acceptance framework comes in handy. This way program management and scrum masters can keep track of what are the requirements that they provided, how they are getting translated across the dev and QA org. This information provides a clear exit strategy for the project, thus resulting in a shorter duration for the whole project to manage.

While test automation is not a new concept and has been around for decades now, BDD ties end-to-end test acceptance into the big picture, thus resulting in a more streamlined process for program management. Automated tests were exclusively handled by the test-automator and more often than not, the result of the test does not come out the way that the automator had planned. This would lead to inevitable conflicts between the programming and the testing department, forcing the head of the project to get involved. BDD has found a way to avoid all this chaos and hold up and make it a constantly flowing streamlined process by taking into consideration the needs and wants of the different groups involved in product delivery system and working out a result that can be “accepted” by all of them.

Framework and language

Even though there a number of frameworks that BDD works with, there one framework in particular that has struck a chord with all developers and testers and has become an instant hit, and that is the Cucumber Framework. This is due to the various advantages that one gets to enjoy while using Cucumber such as integrations, language bindings and various kinds of help resources. Also, the fact that the implementations in the Cucumber Framework can be done in a number of programming languages such as Java, PHP and Ruby also plays in its favor.

Cucumber is perfectly compatible with Gherkin, which is the magical engine that helps display all the codes in a language that can be readable by coders and non-coders alike. Gherkin generally makes use of few commonly known English terminologies such as Given, When and Then to conduct the basics of coding. When such coding is being double-checked by non-technical experts such as the product manager, it saves the coders a lot of time; since these programs are pretty much self-explanatory, that is, if one learns the purpose that each of these terminologies serve, the coders need not explain the function of each and every line of coding.
